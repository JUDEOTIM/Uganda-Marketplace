Step 1

Start Small

Begin with 20–50 chickens to reduce risk and gain experience.

Why start small?

  • Easier management
  • Lower startup cost
  • Opportunity to learn before scaling
💡 Example: Start with 30 birds, learn feeding, disease control, and marketing → then expand to 100+.
A small, manageable starter flock
Step 2

Choose the Right Breed

Your goal determines the type of chicken:

  • Layers: For egg production — start laying at ~5 months
  • Broilers: For meat — ready in 6–8 weeks
  • Kuroiler / Local breeds: Best for beginners

Why Kuroiler / local breeds?

  • Resistant to diseases
  • Can survive on mixed feeding
  • Dual-purpose (eggs + meat)
  • Require less intensive feeding
Choosing the right breed is your first big decision
Step 3

Poultry Housing

Proper housing ensures healthy growth and protection.

  • Good ventilation (fresh air prevents disease)
  • Dry flooring — use wood shavings, not sawdust
  • Protection from predators (dogs, snakes, thieves)
  • Adequate space — 0.1–0.2 m² per bird
💡 Tip: Use the deep litter system — cheap and effective for beginners.
Well-ventilated housing keeps birds healthy
Step 4

Clean Water Supply

Chickens drink more than they eat — water is critical for survival and production.

  • Provide fresh water every day
  • Clean drinkers regularly
  • Avoid any contamination
  • Change water daily — dirty water causes diarrhoea that can wipe out your flock
Clean, full drinkers every single morning
Step 5

Feeding

Feed is your biggest cost (60–70%), so manage it carefully.

  • Starter feed (0–4 weeks)
  • Grower feed (4–8 weeks)
  • Layer mash (for egg production)
  • Feed 2–3 times daily and minimise waste
  • Supplement with greens for local breeds
💡 Formulating your own feed from maize, soya, and fish meal can cut feed costs by up to 30%.
Consistent, measured feeding drives fast growth
Step 6

Health & Vaccination

Disease is the biggest threat in poultry farming. Prevent rather than treat.

Prevention:

  • Vaccinate regularly (Newcastle, Gumboro, IB)
  • Maintain cleanliness inside the house
  • Limit visitors — biosecurity saves lives
⚠️ Common killers: Newcastle Disease, Gumboro, and Coccidiosis. Vaccinate on schedule from Day 7.
Daily health checks catch problems early
Step 7

Market Planning

Plan how you will sell before you start — don't wait until harvest day.

Where to sell:

  • Local markets and trading centres
  • Restaurants & hotels (higher price)
  • Neighbours and community members
  • Shops and supermarkets
💡 Eggs provide daily income. Broilers provide bulk income after 6–8 weeks.
Find your buyer before your birds are ready
Step 8

Basic Budget Idea

Start with what you can afford — don't over-invest early.

  • Chicks: $1–$2 each (UGX 3,500–7,000)
  • Feed: the biggest ongoing cost (~65%)
  • Housing: depends on local materials
  • Vaccines & drugs: budget 5–10% of total
💡 A 30-bird broiler batch can return your full investment in the first cycle if managed well.
